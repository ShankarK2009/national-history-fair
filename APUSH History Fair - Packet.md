# APUSH History Fair - Packet

## Project Title

Terror's Turning Point: The KKK and the Collapse of Reconstruction

## Research Question

How did the Ku Klux Klan's violent backlash against Radical Reconstruction's legal reforms, such as the 14th and 15th Amendments and federal enforcement laws, attempt to reverse post Civil War gains for Black Americans, and how did this resistance contribute both to the collapse of Reconstruction's civil rights protections and to the long-term direction of future civil rights policy in the United States?

## Thesis

Although Radical Reconstruction tried to create lasting constitutional change, white resistance and government withdrawal contributed to ending these efforts. This period left a long legacy of discrimination and delayed the unfulfilled promise of freedom and equality until the Civil Rights Movement of the 1960s.

**Step 1: Research Notes:**

1.  **Political Motivations for Violence:** York County, South Carolina, became a central hub for Ku Klux Klan activity specifically to counter the rising political power of Black citizens and the Republican Party's success in the 1868 state and national elections.
2.  **Backlash Against Black Self-Defense:** The violence escalated significantly in 1870 after Republican Governor Robert Scott appointed three all-Black militias; while these militias successfully protected Black voters during the 1870 election, their presence triggered a massive increase in Klan atrocities.
3.  **Scale of Terror:** A federal investigation led by Col. Lewis Merrill in March 1871 revealed the systematic nature of the resistance, documenting evidence of at least seven murders and over 300 whippings committed by the Klan since the previous autumn.
4.  **Federal Legal Intervention:** Interpreting the violence as a rebellion against the U.S., Congress passed the Third Enforcement Act, which allowed President Ulysses S. Grant to suspend the writ of habeas corpus and declare martial law in nine counties to suppress the Klan.
5.  **Class Disparity in Justice:** Although there were over 1,300 indictments for Klan activities in South Carolina, wealthy leaders managed to flee the region to avoid capture, leaving mostly farmers and laborers to face conviction and imprisonment.
6.  **Abandonment of Enforcement:** Despite the federal crackdown and the imprisonment of some Klansmen in New York, racial violence persisted in York County, and the federal commitment to civil rights protections collapsed when President Grant pardoned all convicted Klansmen by 1876.

**Step 2: Analysis:**

By documenting the specific atrocities in York County, this source provides concrete evidence of the KKK's strategy to violently suppress the Black vote and overthrow the local revolution of biracial governance. The narrative demonstrates the temporary nature of federal enforcement; while military intervention briefly checked the violence, the eventual pardoning of Klansmen reveals the waning political will that allowed reactionary forces to triumph. This supports the thesis by showing how the failure to sustain justice led to the collapse of Reconstruction's protections at the local level.

**Step 1: Research Notes:**

1.  **Systematic Voter Suppression:** In 1868 alone, the Freedmen's Bureau cataloged 336 cases of murder or attempted murder in Georgia, illustrating the scale of the violence used to strip newly enfranchised Black citizens of the voting rights granted by the 14th and 15th Amendments. This systemic terror effectively neutralized federal enforcement laws, as the Klan was able to overpower federal troops at some polling places to prevent Black participation.
2.  **Political Overthrow via Intimidation:** The Klans tactics successfully reversed political gains in Georgia, where intimidation caused Republicans to lose dozens of seats to Democrats between April and November 1868. The terror was so effective that some duly elected Black and white Republican officials were too frightened to take their seats or were forced to resign, directly facilitating the collapse of the biracial governments established by Reconstruction.
3.  **Psychological Warfare and "Ghost" Narratives:** The primary source letter to Davie Jeems, a Black Republican sheriff, demonstrates the Klans use of psychological warfare by adopting the personas of dead Confederate soldiers to terrify victims. By claiming to be "ghosts" returning from battles like Manassas, the Klan sought to culturally legitimize their violence as a supernatural continuation of the Civil War against "radicals."
4.  **Targeting of Black Officials:** The specific threat against Davie Jeems ("We nail all, radicals up in Boxes") highlights the Klan's strategy of targeted assassination and coercion against Black leadership. By threatening to kill Black officials who did not "vote right with the white people" and join the Democratic Party, the Klan forced a binary choice between political abandonment of civil rights or death.
5.  **Restoration of Antebellum Social Hierarchy:** Beyond politics, the Klan utilized violence to reverse social gains by attacking schools, churches, and relief workers to force free Black Southerners back into a state of "servitude." The document notes that anyone perceived as "insolent" or failing to show deference to white supremacy was beaten or killed, thereby socially nullifying the legal equality promised by Reconstruction.
6.  **Paramilitary Organization against Federal Law:** The document suggests that Confederate General Nathan Bedford Forrest used secret meetings to organize the Klan in Georgia, transforming it into a formal paramilitary wing of the Democratic Party. This organized structure allowed the Klan to wage a coordinated insurgency against "carpetbaggers" (Northern allies) and the federal government, ultimately eroding the national will to enforce civil rights protections.

**Step 2: Analysis:**

This primary source letter explicitly documents the psychological and physical terror used by the KKK to intimidate Black officials, effectively dismantling the political revolution of Reconstruction at the grassroots level. By demanding a return to "servitude" and threatening death for political participation, the document exemplifies the violent reaction designed to reestablish white supremacy and nullify federal laws. The source directly supports the thesis by proving that the collapse of civil rights was not accidental but the result of a coordinated campaign to force Black citizens into political submission.

**Type of source: Primary**

**Step 1: Research Notes:**

1.  **Historical Context:** United States v. Cruikshank (1876) arose from the Colfax Massacre (1873), in which more than 100 Black Republicans were murdered by white supremacist groups in Louisiana attempting to overturn Reconstruction-era elections.
2.  **Federal Charges:** The federal government prosecuted perpetrators under the Enforcement Acts, arguing that the violence violated the 14th and 15th Amendments by denying Black citizens equal protection and voting rights.
3.  **Supreme Court Ruling:** The Court ruled that the 14th Amendment only restricted state governments, not private individuals, and therefore the federal government lacked authority to prosecute KKK-style violence.
4.  **Impact on Enforcement Acts:** This decision severely weakened the Enforcement Acts by stripping the federal government of the power to protect Black citizens from domestic terrorist organizations like the KKK.
5.  **Encouragement of White Supremacist Violence:** The ruling effectively signaled to groups such as the KKK and White League that they could act with near-total impunity if state governments refused to intervene.
6.  **Long-Term Legal Consequences:** Cruikshank became a foundational precedent used to justify the rollback of federal civil rights enforcement until the mid-20th century Civil Rights Movement.

**Step 2: Analysis**

The analysis of United States v. Cruikshank highlights the critical judicial failure that crippled the federal government's ability to defend the constitutional revolution against private terror groups like the KKK. By ruling that the Enforcement Acts could not reach private individuals, the Supreme Court effectively legalized the KKK's violent reaction, leaving Black citizens defenseless against local white supremacists. This decision supports the thesis by marking the definitive point where legal protections collapsed, institutionalizing a legacy of violence that would defer justice until the Civil Rights Movement.

**Type of source: Secondary**

**Step 1: Research Notes:**

1.  **Legal Context:** Historians identify Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) as one of the most consequential Supreme Court decisions interpreting the 13th and 14th Amendments during the post-Reconstruction era.
2.  **Court's Ruling:** The Supreme Court upheld Louisiana's Jim Crow law requiring racial segregation on railway cars, establishing the doctrine of "separate but equal."
3.  **Connection to Earlier Decisions:** The ruling built directly on The Civil Rights Cases (1883), which had already limited federal power by ruling Congress could not regulate private racial discrimination.
4.  **Impact on Reconstruction:** Scholars argue that Plessy completed the legal dismantling of Reconstruction-era civil rights by validating state-enforced racial segregation.
5.  **Normalization of White Supremacy:** The decision legitimized segregation as constitutional, allowing the racial hierarchy that the Ku Klux Klan violently defended to become embedded in law.
6.  **Long-Term Legacy:** The ruling remained the legal foundation of Jim Crow segregation until it was overturned by Brown v. Board of Education in 1954.

**Step 2: Analysis:**

Although occurring after the official end of Reconstruction, this source illustrates the long-term legal consolidation of the white supremacist reaction that began with KKK violence. By validating "separate but equal," the Supreme Court formalized the social hierarchy that the Klan had fought to restore, effectively reversing the 14th Amendment's promise of a constitutional revolution. This supports the thesis by demonstrating that the successful intimidation and violence of the Reconstruction era paved the way for the institutionalized segregation that delayed true democratic reform for decades.

# History Fair Outline

**Introduction Paragraph:**
Prior to the Civil War, slavery and racial inequality shaped nearly every part of American life, especially in the South. Politics, the economy, and social interactions revolved around a system built on forced labor and white supremacy. Although Union's victory in 1865, along with the Thirteenth Amendment, officially ended slavery, questions about Black citizenship and political rights remained unclear. During Radical Reconstruction, Congressional Republicans pushed through the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments in order to protect the civil rights and voting rights of those who had just been freed, sparking fierce backlash. This led to the formation of White supremacist groups like the Ku Klux Klan, using violence and intimidation to block reforms, scare Black voters, and attack Republican officials. As federal enforcement declined and the Supreme Court limited Reconstruction's legal support, this campaign of violence became increasingly effective. The KKK and similar groups managed to reverse many of the Reconstruction efforts, crushing civil rights protections and causing Black citizens to lose their political influence. **Although Radical Reconstruction tried to create lasting constitutional change, white resistance and government withdrawal contributed to ending these efforts. This period left a long legacy of discrimination and delayed the unfulfilled promise of freedom and equality until the Civil Rights Movement of the 1960s.**

**Topic Overview:**

- **I. Who:** Newly freed Black Americans, republican leaders, and white supremacist groups, especially the KKK
- **II. What:** An era of violence in order to destroy the reconstruction's legal protections for African Americans, such as the 14th and 15th amendments, and reestablish white supremacy
- **III. When:** During reconstruction (~1865-1877) which effects of KKKs violent reactions leading up to the Civil Rights movements in the 1960s, where African Americans finally achieved the dream of equal rights and citizenship
- **IV. Where:** Mostly the Southern part of the USA, especially in states like Tennessee, South Carolina, Georgia, and Louisiana

**Theme/Sub-claim 1:** The radical reconstruction era attempted a "Constitutional Revolution" to dismantle the antebellum social order and enforce Black citizenship through federal power.

- **A. Evidence:** To combat the violent insurgency of the KKK, the federal government attempted to enforce the "Revolution" of Black citizenship through the Enforcement Acts of 1870 and 1871 (also known as the KKK Acts). These laws allowed the President to suspend habeas corpus and use the military to protect voting rights, marking a massive expansion of federal power to protect civil rights. (Source: Senate.gov, "U.S. Senate: Landmark Legislation")
- **Analysis/Connection to Theme:** These acts represent the "Engine of Change" by the federal government to secure the constitutional revolution of the 14th and 15th Amendments. However, the necessity of such extreme measures (martial law) highlights the intensity of the "Reaction." The government was forced to treat the KKK not just as criminals, but as an insurrection against the new social order.

- **B. Evidence:** In York County, South Carolina, the KKK launched a systematic terror campaign to counter the rising political power of Black citizens. While a federal investigation documented over 300 whippings and murders, and military intervention briefly checked the violence, the commitment to this "Reform" waned. Eventually, President Grant pardoned convicted Klansmen by 1876. (Source: Campbell, "Racial Violence in York County")
- **Analysis/Connection to Theme:** This evidence illustrates the limits of the Reconstruction "Revolution." While federal intervention was initially a strong revolutionary tool, the eventual pardons signaled a retreat. The "Reaction" (KKK violence) proved more durable than the federal will to enforce "Reform," leading to the abandonment of Black citizens in the South.

**Theme/Sub-claim 2:** The KKK led a systematic violent "Reaction" designed to overwhelm federal law and restore white supremacy through psychological and physical terror.

- **A. Evidence:** The KKK used direct psychological warfare and death threats against Black officials to force them out of politics. A primary source letter to Black sheriff Davie Jeems explicitly threatened to "nail all radicals in boxes" unless they stopped supporting the Republican party, demanding they "vote right with the white people." (Source: Groff, "Notice From The Ku Klux Klan To Davie Jeems")
- **Analysis/Connection to Theme:** This demonstrates that the "Reaction" was not random violence, but a calculated political strategy. By forcing a choice between death or abandoning their rights, the KKK aimed to nullify the "Revolution" of the 15th Amendment (voting rights) at the grassroots level, turning the promise of democracy into a liability for Black citizens.
- **B. Evidence:** A Congressional report from 1870 detailed how Klan violence in Tennessee successfully invalidated election results. Governor Brownlow had to throw out original returns because Klan intimidation was so widespread that it distorted the democratic process, forcing the government to intervene to certify the Republican winner. (Source: Mazzeno, "A Contested Election")
- **Analysis/Connection to Theme:** This evidence supports the thesis by proving the "Reaction" successfully overwhelmed the political process. The KKK's terror effectively stripped the ballot box of its power, creating a scenario where the legal "Reform" of voting rights existed on paper but was violently suppressed in practice, leading to the collapse of biracial governance.

**Theme/Sub-claim 3:** The failure of reform and its long term effects

- **A. Evidence:** In United States v Cruikshank, an 1876 court case that was a result of the Colfax massacre, the Supreme Court ruled that the 14th amendment allowed the federal gov. to only act against only state governments, not against private individuals, such as those from terrorist groups like the KKK. Source: "United States v. Cruikshank (1876)"
- **Analysis/Connection to Theme:** This ruling almost fully ended the revolution of reconstruction. This is because it almost fully stripped the federal government's power to prosecute KKK violence, legitimizing the KKKs violent reaction. Therefore, federal protections collapsed, and Black citizens again became vulnerable to racial violence.
- **B. Evidence:** After Reconstruction ended, the court case Plessy v. Ferguson in 1896 approved racial segregation under the "separate but equal" ideology. This ingrained white supremacy in law, validating the racist efforts of the KKK and other supremacist groups until the Brown v. Board of Education court case overturned it in 1954. Source: "Plessy v Ferguson"
- **Analysis/Connection to Theme:** Plessy v. Ferguson validated the long-term victory of the KKKs reaction against reconstruction. Violence from racist groups such as the KKK in the 1870s helped make legal discrimination widespread in the 1890s. Additionally, Civil Rights reform was stopped and rolled back due to this, creating segregation for decades to come.

**IV. Legacy:**
The KKKs violent reaction not only ended reconstruction, but also significantly impacted US history. By using terror to undermine the 14th and 15th amendments, and by pushing the federal courts to submit to racism, the KKK helped usher in a system of racial oppression which lasted for almost a century. Because the policies brought about in the 1860s were somewhat nullified by the violent reaction of the KKK, the African American dream of equal citizenship was much delayed until the Civil Rights movement of the 1960s.
